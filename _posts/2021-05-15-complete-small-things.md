You ever find yourself thinking about something you need to do, like a chore? Following thoughts are on how much effort it'll take to do The Thing.
<!--more-->

Sometimes, The Thing looms over you like a large pile of responsibility you wish you didn't have. And then you get yourself so psyched out, that you're already mentally exhausted - and you end up not working on The Thing.

However, for the unlucky, The Thing might need to be handled by a certain time. By the end of the month? In two weeks? A whole year? It doesn't help that The Thing already wears us down, but as time ticks forward, it gets heavier. You feel it in your chest, your back, your shoulders, and definitely in your head - figuratively and physically.

Then, as the time of reckoning approaches, you may find yourself putting pedal to the metal in order to finally deal with The Thing. Three things can result:

1. You deal with The Thing. Congrats! It was tough but sleepless nights and high-tension stress got you over the wall.

2. You don't deal with The Thing. The Thing has become a bigger Thing. Sometimes slightly. Sometimes doubly. Chances are you have a new deadline, whether it's determined or acknowledged by you or not.

3. You don't deal with The Thing completely, but you got some of it down. The Thing has become smaller, but The Thing is still there. You get a new deadline, again, whether determined or acknowledged by you or not.

So, after not resulting in option 1 for too long, I thought about it. At work, when I didn't break down a sprint goal into appropriate details, I would become overwhelmed with building out a feature while coding almost blindly. Sometimes, rarely even asking for help. As expected, having an ego as a software engineer does no favor.

The advice I got from more experienced engineers varied. I would be told things about how the Product and Project Managers could be doing a better job, or I'd be lectured on the importance of understanding the codebase I'm working in. The former? Yeah that's a possible thing that happens. The latter? There is value of understanding the code you're working within, but I think the solution to dealing with The Thing in a timely manner is much simpler.

When you take a look at The Thing, you realize that it's made up of many smaller Things. And some of those Things are made up of even tinier Things.

None of these smaller Things evoke the same fear as looking at The Thing as a whole. In fact, you may even look at one of the Things by itself and be able to estimate how long it'll take do deal with it. If not, then at the very least dealing with this smaller Thing is doable.

Then you start taking one of the smaller Things. Then another. Soon, the original Thing has decreased in size. With that, it loses some of its negative influence on you.

Piece by piece, The Thing goes away. You are finally done.

This problem would cross paths with me throughout my career - namely in the form of being given a task. Because I would wait so long to start tackling it seriously with thought to planning, my pull requests could potentially be large and cumbersome. Potential reviewers would look at the number of files changed and edited lines, and they'd either sanely close out of the tab to go back to their docs, IDE, or YouTube, or they'd stare at the green and red highlighted text under the glaze of their monitor's brightness.

The worst case scenario is getting a pull request approved, change deployed, and your shipped code doesn't survive its inevitable impact with actual users. Some users end up needing to get refunds since your deployed code prevented them from getting the access they purchased. A page is down that has high traffic, and users hop on Twitter to @ your company with screenshots of the 404. 

The Thing that needed to be done has become The Thing the Company is worried about and knows your name is attached to it. Yikes.

Nobody wants that to happen. So take into consideration how big your changes are. Work in chunks. Break The Thing down into smaller Things. Each smaller Thing taken care of is one less Thing to worry about. Plus, the effort in understanding a small Thing is far less than trying to figure out the bigger Thing.

If you didn't know how to build a table in a MySQL database, and you were tasked to do so, think about how you'd break down the effort in learning what was necessary. Ask yourself questions such as: How do you build a MySQL table in general? What data is it recording? What should be the primary key, column names, etc? What will touch this table? What do you have to do to build a table in the database at your workplace? How do you connect to said database in the first place? Who needs to be notified about this change?

The answers to questions like that are all Things that will contribute in dealing with The Thing of building out this hypothetical MySQL table. Every time you come up with an answer, or something noteworthy, document it.

You're now one step closer than you were before. Your code reviewers are able to be more thorough, and your changes are more consumable and palpable.

Then, when all the Things are out the way, you're finally done.
