Departure

Last year, I quit my software engineering job. I was making the most money I've ever made in my life, and it's likely I won't ever reach that sort of income again for a while if at all, but I had already started counting down the months, weeks, and days until I was going to step away. I had invested about a decade into my career, and I found myself at wit's end.

More than not, there was stress. But stress isn't a bad thing. Anything worth it will be difficult to achieve or accomplish. So a stress-free experience in life is an impossibility. The stress I'd encounter centered around keeping up with assigned tickets, being considered a lead developer and keeping up with the other lead developers in both code contribution and being that legendary 'force multiplier' for your team. The latter being what your manager wanted; the former being what was baseline.

What came to a head for me was the purpose of the stress. So the question to ask was: Why was I stressed?

The answer was because I had work I needed to complete. Work needed to be done. Then I asked: Why do I care so much about the work? Because in reality, I didn't. I just wanted to be paid so I could continue saving up and continue to afford my modest lifestyle. I grew up without money, so I developed a certain frugality with a single-track mindset: Get money no matter what.

That was my focus. That had been my only focus, arguably ever since I began to ponder about what I'd do after I was completely done with school. Getting a master's degree didn't seem too financially rewarding once I got my bachelor's. However, any other goal I had in mind fell to the wayside. The question of what I truly wanted to do was shelved. The priority was to get money. So I put my head down into the code and learned enough to work. I only applied to jobs so I could further my career. Out the gate, after graduation, the goal was to find a junior engineering position, which I did in a year.

I didn't even care about the technology. I just wanted to be paid. I wanted to be better off than my family, and I managed to achieve that.

"But to what end?" is what I asked myself once I got a grasp of setting up my retirement accounts. The idea of retiring in my golden years mathematically made sense. However, logic has a tendency to hit you with a fuller picture when you open your eyes for a bit and look outside your own experiences (or rather your own limited understanding of the world). This took the form of my mother - a woman who had dedicated over thirty years of her life to working as a nurse under a number of hospitals. Due to physical complications developed from needing to stand up for long periods of time and carefully handling patients by lifting them out of bed, while doing these things over night shifts, my mother needed to retire early. Her legs were a mess. She couldn't work anymore.

I don't know the whole picture, but I do know she didn't get as much money as she could've if she had worked a year or two more and retired then. But she was touching on sixty, and younger nurses with more advanced nursing degrees than her were literally running circles around her. What purpose did she have left at her work? To get money? That was the answer back when I was a child and lived with her. But I was gone, so what was the point? So she left, and a good chunk of her potential retirement was taken away.

Seeing that unfold made me reflect on my own experience with the reality of being a working adult. Loyalty from working a long time with someone doesn't mean much in the relationship between an organization that runs your workplace and you, the individual human that dedicated years of your life to it. Nobody looked at my mother's experience and figured she was worth an exception despite her making an exception for them. Those years are just as gone as the potential money.

Looking at myself, I asked what was I doing. No, really. What was the purpose of my retirement? To retire with around a million bucks in my 60s? Who's to say I even reach that age? Or even if I do, how much would a million even afford me in those years? Inflation is already doing a number on the population. Nobody can buy a house if they didn't have one already. The Dollar Menu at McDonald's is gone and they're even taking away free refills. If that's not an indicator for the tumultuous nature of monetary value, then I don't know what else to say.

And this is for a field I really didn't care about. As long as I got paid, I did whatever. Day in. Day out. Sometimes it was easy. Sometimes it wasn't. I grew to fiend for the easy days, lunch breaks, and the day to be over. I wanted to cruise.

I didn't care. I cared about getting the job and doing just enough to be paid. But I didn't care. Didn't care about the job. Didn't care about the profession aside from my own reputation.

Meanwhile, the world is burning. Kids are becoming stupider while being raised on social media if they're not outright dying. Food is becoming more artificial, expensive, and unhealthy. Companies have gotten so big that they must regurgitate whatever has historically made the biggest returns as much as they can while nickel-and-diming as many people as they can through all manners of subscriptions and piecemeal transactions. The government is 10 years behind on doing anything substantial for its citizens all while devoting resources to maintain international relationships that may or may not contradict fundamental tenets the government itself was built upon.

And for me? I shelved whatever hopes, dreams, and interests I had in order to chase a dollar the 'right' way. Go to school. Get a job. Get paid.

So I left my career.

Hiatus

In the time I've been away from working, I've dabbled here and there in a few things. I've acknowledged I have a void within me. That drive I once had in making things is a lot harder to muster; I'm so used to being assigned a task and needing to flesh it out. Then there's that toxic, persistent desire for perfection.

I took up drawing for a bit. I got better at it than I ever was, but paused. It's something I really should get back into. It was fun when I was feeling it.

I also got a bit more into game development. I even managed to make a few small browser games that may or may not see the light of day since they're extremely rough, but I'm proud of them since I did take the time to make them from scratch.

But what has lasted the longest and has been the most consistent has been my focus on my physical health. The day I left my job, I got into jogging regularly. I started with Couch to 5K, completed that, and here I am now jogging three days a week. It's become a habit I never thought I would have.

I also got back into weightlifting. I'm not as strong as I used to be back in my early twenties, but I have more energy (though my knees remind me how much time really has passed).

I'm a healthier person. I've finally started to knock out that excess weight I obtained during the pandemic and now I'm down forty pounds with more of a tone I haven't had since, again, my college years.

But even I know this is borrowed time. I've been living off my savings, which I hate thinking about.

Currently, I've been hunting for a new job. But at the same time, I'm exploring alternatives. Freelancing is one angle I'd like to gain experience in. Given the skills I have from being a professional engineer, it seems I could shift them towards a personal freelancing business if I focused on it.

I also began pulling old goals off the metaphorical shelves to see if the current version of myself can bring them into fruition. Being able to produce a deliverable and make even a cent off it would be a success in my eyes. A cent is more than nothing, and being paid for your ideas would be a new kind of achievement.
